The escalation timeline — what they do, and when Days 1–30 Stage 1 Calls, letters, and late notices begin immediately The resort begins automated calls, certified letters, and past-due notices within 30 days of a missed payment. These come to your home address, phone, and email on file. The tone starts polite — payment arrangements, grace period offers — but the frequency increases every week you don't respond. Day 30 Stage 2 Credit bureau reporting begins — all three At 30 days past due, Capital Vacations reports the delinquency to Equifax, Experian, and TransUnion simultaneously. This is not a threat — it is automatic. Your credit score drops the day that report hits. The longer it stays unpaid, the worse the damage. Most owners see a 100–150 point drop in the first 90 days alone. 60–90 Days Stage 3 Debt transferred to a third-party collection agency The resort sells or transfers your delinquent account to a collections agency. Now you have a second entity reporting against your credit — the resort AND the collection company. You will receive collection calls, collection letters, and a second negative mark on your credit report that is independent of the resort's reporting. Both stay on your record. 90–180 Days Stage 4 Formal foreclosure threats in writing Because a timeshare is classified as real property — just like a house — the resort has the legal right to foreclose on it. At this stage you begin receiving formal foreclosure notices. These are not scare tactics. Resorts follow through. The foreclosure process is now formally initiated and your case is handed to their legal department or outside counsel. 180–360 Days Stage 5 This is where your situation determines their strategy At this point, Capital Vacations — like most resort chains — makes a calculated decision based on your age and financial profile. This is the fork in the road that most owners don't know about.
